Brex, Ramp, and Mercury have collectively become the default banking infrastructure for venture-backed startups. They are not the same product. They were built to solve different problems at different company stages, and choosing the wrong one early creates friction that takes engineering time and operational disruption to undo.

## Quick verdict

**Choose Mercury** if you are an early-stage startup wanting the best free business bank account with no monthly fees, a clean API, and straightforward FDIC-insured banking.

**Choose Ramp** if you have a team spending money on SaaS, travel, and vendors and you want a platform that actively finds savings, enforces spend controls, and automates expense reconciliation.

**Choose Brex** if you are Series A or beyond and want high credit limits without a personal guarantee, strong travel rewards, and a combined card and banking product designed for high-growth companies.

Many fast-growing companies use Mercury for banking and Ramp for expense management simultaneously.

## What each was built to solve

**Mercury** was built as a bank account for startups that traditional banks would not take seriously. Before Mercury, founders with a Delaware C-corp, no revenue, and six months of runway often struggled to open a business bank account at Chase or Wells Fargo. Mercury solved this: clean online application, approval within days, no minimum balance, no monthly fees, and a proper API that integrates with accounting software.

**Ramp** was built on a specific thesis: companies waste a predictable percentage of their SaaS spend on unused subscriptions, duplicate tools, and unoptimised vendor contracts. Ramp's core pitch is not a better corporate card — it is that they will save you money. The platform monitors spend, identifies waste, and surfaces savings recommendations. It enforces spend controls: department budgets, merchant blocking, approval workflows, and real-time visibility.

**Brex** started as a corporate card for startups that could not get credit. Traditional corporate cards require a personal guarantee from the founder. Brex underwrote cards against company cash balance and funding stage instead, offering limits that scaled with runway rather than the founder's personal credit score.

## Credit limits and underwriting

**Brex** offers credit limits that can reach into the millions for well-funded companies. No personal guarantee is required — ever. For a founder with $5 million in the bank from a Series A, Brex can offer limits far above what any traditional corporate card would provide based on the company's short operating history.

**Ramp** underwrites against business cash balance without a personal guarantee. Limits are typically 1.5-2% of your bank balance. For a company with $2 million in the bank, the initial Ramp limit might be $30,000-$40,000, increasing as the relationship develops.

**Mercury** offers the Mercury Credit Card as a charge card requiring full monthly repayment. It is a simpler product with lower limits, but appropriate for companies primarily needing the banking account with a card for routine expenses.

## Fees

**Mercury** has no monthly fees on its core product. Wire transfer fees are $5 domestic and $20 international on the standard plan, waived on Mercury Plus at $35/month. For a company primarily using ACH transfers, the effective monthly cost is zero.

**Ramp** has no monthly fees. It makes money from interchange fees paid by merchants. Ramp Plus, which adds advanced approval workflows and deeper ERP integrations, costs $15 per user per month.

**Brex** moved to a paid model for its full product suite. Brex Essentials is available at no monthly fee. Brex Premium, which includes expense management and dedicated support, is priced per user. At scale, Brex is the most expensive of the three.

## Rewards and cashback

**Brex** offers strong rewards for startup spending patterns: 7x on rideshare, 4x on travel booked through Brex Travel, 3x on restaurants, 2x on software subscriptions. For a company spending heavily on AWS, travel, and SaaS, Brex points accumulate quickly.

**Ramp** explicitly de-emphasises rewards. Its argument is that optimising for cashback percentages distracts from the larger opportunity of eliminating wasteful spend. Ramp offers 1.5% cashback on all purchases — simple and consistent.

**Mercury** offers 1.5% cashback on its credit card for qualifying spend.

## Integrations and accounting

All three integrate with QuickBooks, Xero, and NetSuite. The depth differs significantly.

**Ramp's** accounting integrations are the most feature-rich. Automatic categorisation, receipt matching, and GL coding reduce the monthly close from days to hours for companies that configure it properly. Ramp claims customers reduce time spent on expense management by 70-80% after full implementation.

**Brex** integrates well with major accounting platforms and adds Brex Travel, keeping all spend in one platform and simplifying reconciliation for companies with significant travel budgets.

**Mercury's** integrations are solid for a banking product but less sophisticated than Ramp's dedicated expense management tooling.

## Who should use what

**Pre-seed and seed (under 10 people, limited monthly spend):** Mercury. Free account, simple application, clean infrastructure. You are not yet at a stage where Ramp's analytics deliver meaningful savings or Brex's high limits are relevant.

**Series A and beyond with growing team spend:** Add Ramp. Keep Mercury for banking, route company card spend through Ramp for visibility and controls.

**Later stage with significant travel budget and need for high credit limits:** Brex earns its place. The rewards on travel and software, the high limits without personal guarantee, and dedicated support make it right at scale.

There is no wrong answer at the early stage. The wrong answer is staying with a traditional bank business account when Mercury exists.
